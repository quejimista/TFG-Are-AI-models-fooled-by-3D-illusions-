## Project Summary

Monocular Depth Estimation (MDE) models reconstruct 3D spatial structure from a single 2D image.
Because they are trained on large photographic datasets, they implicitly acquire statistical
regularities about the visual world, known as priors. These priors allow them to function well
under normal conditions, but they also make the models vulnerable to inputs that violate the
expected regularities.

This project investigates that vulnerability systematically using 3D optical illusions as
diagnostic stimuli. Optical illusions are not visual malfunctions; they are inputs that expose
the predictive rules a system uses to interpret ambiguous data. By applying them to MDE models,
this work maps the internal priors of each architecture and quantifies how those priors have
evolved across model generations.

The models evaluated are the Depth Anything family (V1, V2, and V3), which use Vision
Transformer (ViT) backbones, and MiDaS, which uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)
backbone. Together they cover two distinct architectural paradigms and three generations of
the same model lineage.

## Research Questions and Contributions

The central question driving this project is: how do Vision Foundation Models understand
different types of 3D optical illusions, and do their failures mirror those of the human
visual system?

To answer it, the following contributions are made:

**Curated illusion taxonomy.** A dataset of 3D optical illusions is organised into five
perceptual categories: impossible objects, forced perspective, depth ambiguity, converging
lines (linear perspective), and shading and illumination (shape-from-shading). The categories
follow Gregory's framework for classifying visual paradoxes and are not mutually exclusive.

**Synthetic Stimuli Engineering.** A controlled ablation study over the Corridor Illusion
is developed. Fifteen variants are generated by systematically removing or modifying
individual visual features (walls, shadows, textures, frequency content, semantic content)
to isolate the specific cues that trigger depth biases in each model.

**Artificial Psychophysics framework.** Borrowing from classical psychophysics, each model
is treated as a perceptual agent and exposed to 30-frame stimulus sequences in which a
single parameter is varied incrementally. Two quantitative thresholds are extracted from
the resulting depth curves: the Point of Subjective Equality (PSE), the stimulus level at
which the model perceives two equidistant objects as equally deep, and the Just Noticeable
Difference (JND), the minimum stimulus change required for the model to alter its depth
assignment significantly.

**Voxel-based cross-domain laboratory.** Key illusions are reconstructed in Minecraft, a
voxel engine that eliminates organic textures and high-frequency noise. This allows the
geometric structure of each illusion to be tested in isolation, confirming whether the
identified priors are texture-dependent or architecture-level properties.

**Cross-architecture comparison.** MiDaS is evaluated on the same experimental protocol
as the Depth Anything models, enabling a direct comparison between CNN-based and
ViT-based depth estimation and a test of whether specific priors are tied to architectural
choices.

## Datasets

### Curated illusion dataset

The baseline dataset in `data/dataset/` contains 34 images organised into five categories.
Each category tests a distinct type of spatial prior:

- **Converging lines:** Corridor Illusion, Ponzo Illusion, Kanizsa Triangle, Muller-Lyer Illusion
- **Depth ambiguity:** Necker Cube, Schroeder Stairs, Dazzle Camouflage, Eiffel Tower
- **Forced perspective:** Ames Room, Beuchet Chair, Salar de Uyuni, Anamorphic Hole, Escaping Criticism (Borrell del Caso), Leaning Tower
- **Impossible objects:** Penrose Triangle, Escher Cube, Escher Waterfall, Blivet Trident, Reutersvard Triangle
- **Shading and illumination:** Gardner Dragon, Hollow Face, Crater Dome, Cornsweet Illusion, Kersten Shadow Effect, Zebra Crossing, Coloured Circles (Akiyoshi)

### Synthetic Corridor Illusion variants

The fifteen variants in `data/synthetic/corridor_illusion/` form a structured ablation:

| Variant | Category | What is tested |
|---|---|---|
| base_image | Baseline | Full illusion with all cues present |
| no_walls | Structural | Reliance on converging lines |
| cube_walls | Structural | Effect of reinforced lateral boundaries |
| warped | Structural | Robustness to distorted perspective |
| solid | Lighting | Shadow removal |
| solid_bordered | Lighting | Object border as grounding cue |
| cylinder_pattern | Augmentation | Checkerboard texture on cylinders |
| cylinder_pattern_bordered | Augmentation | Bordered checkerboard |
| no_cyllinders (Venus) | Semantic | Object identity (semantic content) |
| only_cylinders | Semantic | Depth prediction with no environmental context |
| size_prior | Semantic | Whether object size overrides position |
| shepard_illusion | Semantic | Effect of horizontal orientation |
| low_pass | Frequency | Low-frequency structural information only |
| high_pass | Frequency | High-frequency edge information only |
| high-low_filter | Frequency | Split-frequency cylinders |

### Voxel laboratory

The Minecraft experiments in `data/minecraft/` reconstruct illusions in a voxel environment
to test whether identified priors depend on photorealistic texture or on geometric structure
alone. Render settings are documented in `data/minecraft/Settings_Configuration.txt`.
Experiments cover all five illusion categories.

### Psychophysics sequences

The sequences in `data/psychophysics/` are generated programmatically by cell 9 of the
main notebook. Each experiment produces 30 frames in which one parameter is varied
linearly (for example, the vertical position of a square, the convergence angle of two
lines, or the distance of a shadow from its object). Fifteen experiments are defined,
covering height bias, Ponzo bias, hollow face bias, occlusion bias, shadow attachment
bias, and others.

## Experimental Pipeline

### Step 1: Inference and depth map generation

Each image is passed through all models using a unified script. The output is a
four-panel comparison image (original, V1, V2, V3 or MiDaS) saved to the corresponding
subfolder of `results/depth_map_comparisons/`.

In all depth maps, brighter pixel intensity encodes closer proximity and darker intensity
encodes greater distance.

### Step 2: ROI analysis and depth gap measurement

The interactive ROI tool (cell 8) allows the user to draw a bounding box on the original
image panel. The same bounding box is automatically applied to all model output panels.
For each region, the mean pixel intensity is extracted.

The primary metric is the Depth Gap:

    delta_I = mean_intensity(ROI_A) - mean_intensity(ROI_B)

where ROI_A and ROI_B correspond to two objects that are ground-truth equidistant.
A non-zero delta_I indicates measurable illusory bias. Values above 60 intensity units
are considered significant. All metrics are exported to CSV files in `results/roi_analysis/`.

### Step 3: Psychophysics

Each 30-frame sequence is processed through the models and the resulting delta_I values
are recorded per frame. The curve is modelled via linear interpolation to compute:

- PSE: the frame at which delta_I crosses zero (the model perceives both ROIs as equidistant)
- JND: the distance from the PSE to the 84% maximum threshold, equivalent to one standard
  deviation of a cumulative Gaussian distribution

A low JND (steep curve) indicates high sensitivity; a high JND indicates robustness.

The cross-model comparison plots (files named comp_* and comparison_*) are saved to
`results/psychophysics/`. These show the PSE and JND curves for all models side by side
and are distinct from the ROI bounding-box outputs in `results/roi_analysis/` and the
raw depth map comparisons in `results/depth_map_comparisons/`.

### Step 4: 3D mesh validation

Selected illusions were submitted to the Hunyuan3D 2.1 generative model (17B parameters)
via Hugging Face ZeroGPU Spaces to produce 3D mesh reconstructions. These serve as
secondary validation: impossible objects that MDE models handle ambiguously are resolved
unambiguously in 3D mesh form, either by hallucination or by topological collapse.
Generated meshes are stored in `results/3d_generations/`.

## Results Summary

The full quantitative results are presented in the report. The key findings are:

**All models are susceptible to 3D illusions.** Depth gaps range from approximately 6 to
148 intensity units across experiments where the ground truth is 0. The susceptibility is
not uniform: forced perspective illusions are largely resisted, while linear perspective
and shading-based illusions produce strong and consistent biases.

**Model biases have evolved across generations.** V1 is governed by a height-in-field
prior, where vertical position is the dominant depth cue. V2 transitions to a centrality
prior, where objects closer to the image centre are predicted as farther. V3 integrates
contextual cues more aggressively, achieving the highest depth gaps when full cues are
present, but collapsing to near zero when structural cues are removed.

**The priors are structural, not textural.** Results in the voxel laboratory are broadly
consistent with those from the photographic dataset, confirming that the identified biases
are architecture-level properties rather than artefacts of photorealistic texture.

**CNN and ViT architectures share a foundational bias.** MiDaS exhibits a height-in-field
prior comparable to Depth Anything V1, suggesting this prior is a general property of
convolution-based depth estimation rather than something specific to the Depth Anything
training regime.

| Model | Primary prior | Robustness (no context) | Susceptibility (full context) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Depth Anything V1 | Height-in-field | Low | Moderate |
| Depth Anything V2 | Centrality | Low | Moderate to high |
| Depth Anything V3 | Global context integration | High | High |
| MiDaS | Height-in-field | Low | Moderate |
